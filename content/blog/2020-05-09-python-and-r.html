In R, you are able to grab specific variables or observations that you are intersted in analyzing. You do this by using “filter” or “select” with the “%” function. Well python can also grab or match what you are interested in as well. I am going to show a couple of examples of how to do that.

import re
x="I am going to match the letter a followed by anything." 
re.findall(r"a.",x)
## ['am', 'at', 'a ', 'an']

As you can see, the output is all of the times “a” is followed by something because the period after the “a” indicated a wildcard which can be anything.

Now, lets make things a little bit more complicated (for someone new to Python at least).

funexample= "You can also match digits. Lets try and match 1,2,3,4,5,6."
re.findall('\d+',funexample)
## ['1', '2', '3', '4', '5', '6']

The output only gave me the digits. This is because backslad followed by “d” grabs digits while the + ensures your grabbing all the digits. I know this might appear to be simple but it actually was confusing when I first tested it out. Why not keep going?
